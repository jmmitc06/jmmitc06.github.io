## About

I'm a computational scientist with an MD/PhD background, working at the intersection of clinical medicine, computational biology, and algorithm design. My career has been shaped by a central conviction: that biological systems encode information in ways that are deeply amenable to mathematical and computational approaches — if you build the right tools.

Over the past decade, I've focused on mass spectrometry-based metabolomics, developing algorithms and pipelines that tackle some of the field's hardest unsolved problems. My recent work, LUNA, is a molecular formula enumeration engine that uses number-theoretic methods and the convolution theorem to scale to 20+ element search spaces where existing tools like SIRIUS and Rdisop cannot. LUNA sits within a broader annotation ecosystem: I extended existing tools like asari (preprocessing) and khipu (pre-annotation), developed SMIRFE (a patented formula inference method published in *Analytical Chemistry*), and built PCPFM, an end-to-end metabolomics data processing pipeline published in *PLOS Computational Biology*.

My work with AI and machine learning runs throughout my career. I published early work demonstrating the potential for accidental data leakage in trained models, and at Los Alamos National Laboratory I applied random forest classifiers, genetic algorithms, and graph coloring methods to solve chemical substructure problems. More recently, I've explored customized GPTs to help non-expert users adopt my metabolomics tools, lowering the barrier to entry for complex computational pipelines.

I'm not just a computational person who works on mass spectrometry data from a distance. At LANL, I built a metabolomics pipeline from the ground up for studying corn — including hands-on bench work with enzyme assays and the analytical methods that generate the data I write software to process. My datasets have spanned human studies, viral, and bacterial systems, giving me a broad perspective on how metabolomics applies across domains. I believe the application of computer science to biology has the potential to solve major problems facing humanity — from food security to disease — which in turn can reduce conflict and improve all human lives.

My medical training — I completed an MD with a focus on primary care — grounds how I think about biological data. Not as abstract signals, but as reflections of real physiology and pathology. That clinical lens continues to inform the tools I build and the questions I ask.

Outside of work, I'm a hardware enthusiast who runs a home cluster for private cloud services and self-hosted LLMs. I also enjoy traveling, recreational mathematics, and gardening with my wife and puppy.

I'm interested in roles where I can apply this combination of clinical intuition, computational depth, and systems-level thinking — whether that's in AI for biology, computational chemistry, or biosafety research.
